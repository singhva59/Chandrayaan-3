Chandrayaan-3 is a follow-on mission to Chandrayaan-2 to demonstrate end-to-end capability in safe landing and roving on the lunar surface. It consists of Lander and Rover configuration. It will be launched by LVM3 from SDSC SHAR, Sriharikota. The propulsion module will carry the lander and rover configuration till 100 km lunar orbit. The propulsion module has Spectro-polarimetry of Habitable Planet Earth (SHAPE) payload to study the spectral and Polari metric measurements of Earth from the lunar orbit.

The third lunar mission performed by the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), Chandrayaan-3, is a ground-breaking venture that intends to advance India's capabilities in space exploration. After the success of its predecessors, Chandrayaan-1 and Chandrayaan-2, ISRO's Chandrayaan-3 mission is prepared to continue the country's quest for lunar knowledge and discoveries.

Mission Profile

Launch

Chandrayaan-3 mission began on July 14 with a launch aboard India's LVM3 rocket, the country's heavy lift vehicle capable of placing about 8 metric tons into low-Earth orbit. (For comparison, the SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket can lift almost 23 metric tons to low-Earth orbit.)

The LVM3 will place the spacecraft and an attached propulsion module into an elongated Earth orbit with an apogee, or high point, of about 36,500 kilometers (22,700 miles) above the planet. The propulsion module will raise its orbit several times before transferring into lunar orbit.

Spacecraft

Chandrayaan-3 is an indigenous Lander module, Propulsion module, and Rover designed to develop and demonstrate interplanetary technologies. Scientific payloads study lunar environment, including quakes, surface thermal properties, plasma changes, and accurate distance measurements between Earth and the moon.Chandrayaan-3, which weighs 3,900kg and cost 6.1bn rupees and following science payloads:

  • Radio Anatomy of Moon Bound Hypersensitive ionosphere and Atmosphere (RAMBHA),Langmuir probe (LP)
  • Chandra's Surface Thermo physical Experiment (ChaSTE)
  • Instrument for Lunar Seismic Activity (ILSA)
  • LASER Retroreflector Array (LRA)
  • LASER Induced Breakdown Spectroscope (LIBS)
  • Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer (APXS)
  • Spectro-polarimetry of HAbitable Planet Earth (SHAPE)

The European Space Agency (ESA) will support the Chandrayaan-3 mission, which will use ISRO's deep space communication antenna. The mission will rely on global ground station support, coordinated by ESA and NASA. ESA's 15 m antenna in Kourou, French Guiana, will track Chandrayaan-3 after launch to ensure its health and survival. Goonhilly Earth Station Ltd in the UK will also provide tracking support. ISRO's Aditya-L1 solar observatory is set to launch in August, and ESA support will include similar activities from Kourou and Goonhilly.

Getting into Lunar orbit

Trans-lunar injection (TLI)

Rocket detaches in two stages ,Chandrayaan-3 will successfully complete a five-day journey to the Moon using a slingshot with a 2145-kg propulsion module and landing module, Vikram and Pragyan, traveling straight to the lunar orbit.

Lunar Orbit Insertion (LOI)

After the TLI, the spacecraft will enter a cruise phase, during which it will travel through space towards the Moon using minimal thrust from its propulsion system.

The spacecraft approaches the Moon, it will execute another critical engine burn known as Lunar Orbit Insertion (LOI). This maneuver will slow down the spacecraft enough to be captured by the Moon's gravity and enter into lunar orbit.

At the Moon, the propulsion module will lower Chandrayaan-3 until it reaches a circular, 100-kilometer (62-mile) orbit.

Once in lunar orbit, the spacecraft will perform periodic orbit maintenance maneuvers to ensure it remains in the intended orbit and avoids collision with the lunar surface.

Soft Landing

The lander and rover will separate from the orbiter and descend towards the lunar surface.

The lander will perform a series of braking maneuvers to achieve a soft landing on the lunar surface. The descent engine will control the speed and trajectory of the lander during this phase.

Lands near the South pole of Moon

Moon Analysis

The six-wheeled rover will then eject and roam around the rocks and craters on Moon's surface, gathering crucial data and images to be sent back to Earth for analysis.

The south pole of the Moon remains unexplored, with a larger shadow area than the north pole. Chandrayaan-1 discovered water near the south pole in 2008, suggesting potential for water in shadowed areas.
